How to sit

  1. Choose a quiet place where you can sit comfortably.
  2. Use a chair, sofa, meditation cushion, or bench. All are fine.
  3. Sit in a way that feels steady but not rigid. It's ok to relax into a sofa, or meditate lying down, or standing, or walking, if this is what your body needs.
  4. Let your hands rest comfortably.
  5. Allow your eyes to be softly open or gently closed.
  6. Take a few, slow, breaths and settle into your breath and your body.
  7. Check for tense muscles, such as in your shoulders or jaw or face, and relax any tension you are holding.
  8. Now you are ready to "Just Sit". Try 5 minutes. Try 10 minutes. Try 20 minutes.
  9. Stretch and move a little before standing up.

Practising with thoughts

In meditation you do not have to stop thinking. When we meditate, the mind wanders over all kinds of territory. We may find ourselves thinking about something that heppened at work, or a conversation with a loved one; we might run over plans, or worries we are having, or dive into old memories We might think about things we wish had gone differently, re-imagining a different, preferrable, outcome.

Our thoughts are revealing: they show us what we worry about; they reveal our obsessions; our patterns of avoidance; our insecurities. They are actually a rich ground and opportunity for us to come to terms with our own life histories and how they influence our current experience of our life.

There can be unresolved grief hiding under repetitive thoughts. Somehow it is easier to run over a story about how a wrong was done to us than to feel into the pain that lies beneath it. One aspect of meditation is learning to feel, and learning to trust that it is ok to feel, that we can survive it.

This is an important aspect of practicing with thoughts: turning out attention to our bodies and the sensations we are experiencing. This experiencing of our emotions in our bodies is a powerful way of processing things we might have tucked away for a long time.

Over time, we tend to become less caught up in our thoughts. We find in meditation that we have themes that we return to over and over. When we begin to recognise this, and we might notice these familiar trains of thought as they comeup. This is very helpful! At this point we have processed some of the emotional charge associated them can begin to feel more relaxed.

A useful practice can be to "label" on our thoughts. In this way we can learn to recognise that thoughts about an experience are not the experience itself. Having a thought about an experience tends to move quickly to re-living the emotions of the experience. Labelling helps us recognise that this is what we are doing.

So when we are thinking "My father was so mean to me", we can shift this, and say to ourselves "Having a thought that my father was so mean to me". Try putting "Having a thought that..." before the thought and notice how it enables us to slightly shift our perception and experience of it.
