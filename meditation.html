Practising ZEN

Practising with thoughts

"With our thoughts we make the world" -- Narrator at the start of the 1970s cult classic television series Monkey Magic.

In meditation you do not have to stop thinking. When we meditate, the mind wanders over all kinds of territory. We may find ourselves thinking about something that happened at work, or a conversation with a loved one; we might day-dream about something we want, we might run over plans, or worries we are having, or we might dive into old memories. We might think about things we wish had gone differently, imagining a different outcome.

Our thoughts are revealing: they show us what we worry about, they reveal our obsessions, our patterns of avoidance, our insecurities. They are actually a rich ground and opportunity for us to come to terms with our own life histories. How we perceive our past has a huge bearing on how we experience our life today. Practice can help us, over time, process our past in a healthier way, with greater perspective and more acceptance of ours and others' mistakes and short-comings.

There can be unresolved grief hiding under repetitive thoughts. Somehow it is easier to run over a story about how a wrong was done to us than to feel into the pain that lies beneath it. One aspect of meditation is learning to feel, and learning to trust that it is ok to feel, that we can survive it.

This is an important aspect of practicing with thoughts: turning out attention to our bodies and the sensations we are experiencing. This experiencing of our emotions in our bodies is a powerful way of processing things we might have tucked away for a long time.

We find in meditation that we have themes that we return to over and over. When we begin to realise this, we might recognise these familiar trains of thought as they come up. This is very helpful! At this point we might begin to experience somewhat less of the emotional charge associated them, and feel less compelled to follow the thoughts. Over time, we do tend to become less caught in "sticky" thoughts.

A useful practice can be to "label" our thoughts. In this way we can learn to recognise the impact of our thoughts, and see that thoughts about an experience are not the experience itself. Thinking about an experience tends to move quickly to re-living all the emotions associated with it. Labelling helps us recognise that this is what we are doing, literally re-creating the past.

So when we are remembering things and thinking "My father was so mean to me," and it is painful, we can slightly shift our point of view by saying to ourselves "Having a thought that my father was so mean to me". Try putting "Having a thought that..." before the words you were thinking and notice how it creates a slight shift in perspective. The more we can come to see thoughts as thoughts the more we understand that our thoughts don't have to hold us hostage.

In the language of Zen, this begins to directs us toward the perception of the emptiness of thoughts. What is being pointed to here is an understanding of the nature of thoughts as lacking fixed or inherent form, lacking substance of any kind.

In general, emptiness in Zen doesn't just mean blankness, or bland nothingness, but rather something akin to: brimming with the potential of utter undefinability. A container that is empty can potentially hold anything. So, what could it mean for things to be fundamentally unable to be defined or pinned down in any way?

Interestingly, these kinds of contemplations are like limbering up or stretching exercises for the mind—like mental tai chi or yoga—because they expand the range of possibilities we are able to see in the situations that present themselves in everyday life.

The practice helps us have more flexibility in our thinking processes and therefor in how we perceive and understand events, and circumstances, and other people, and also ourselves. In this way the practice changes how we think about things and opens the door of compassion and creativity in how we live our lives.

Joko Beck on thoughts

STUDENT: Would you expand upon the idea of letting go of thoughts that occur during meditation?

JOKO: I don’t think that we ever let go of anything. I think what we do is just wear things out. If we start forcing our mind to do something, we are right back into the dualism that we are trying to get out of. The best way to let go is to notice the thoughts as they come up and to acknowledge them. “Oh, yes, I’m doing that one again”—and without judging, return to the clear experience of the present moment. Just be patient. We might have to do it ten thousand times, but the value for our practice is the constant return of the mind into the present, over and over and over. Don’t look for some wonderful place where thoughts won’t occur. Since the thoughts basically are not real, at some point they get dimmer and less imperative and we will find there are periods when they tend to fade out because we see they are not real. They will just wither away in time without our quite knowing how it happened. Those thoughts are our attempt to protect ourselves. None of us really wants to give them up; they are what we are attached to. The way we can eventually see their unreality is by just letting the movie run. After we have seen the same movie five hundred times it gets boring, frankly!

There are two kinds of thoughts. There is nothing wrong with thinking in the sense of what I call “technical thinking.” We have to think in order to walk from here to the corner or to bake a cake or to solve a physics problem. That use of the mind is fine. It isn’t real or unreal; it is just what it is. But opinions, judgments, memories, dreaming about the future—ninety percent of the thoughts spinning around in our heads have no essential reality. And we go from birth to death, unless we wake up, wasting most of our life with them. The gruesome part of sitting (and it is gruesome, believe me) is to begin to see what is really going on in our mind. It is a shocker for all of us. We see that we are violent, prejudiced, and selfish. We are all those things because a conditioned life based on false thinking leads to these states. Human beings are basically good, kind, and compassionate, but it takes hard digging to uncover that buried jewel.
- Charlotte Joko Beck, Everyday Zen
