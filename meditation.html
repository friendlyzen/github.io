Friendly ZEN

Meditation

Friendly Zen meditation is simple. We sit down, settle the body, and allow ourselves to be here.

We are not trying to become calm by force. We are not trying to have a special experience. We are not trying to get rid of our thoughts.

We are practising being with our life as it is. This means our breathing, our body, our feelings, our thoughts, and the ordinary fact of being alive.

Just sitting

In Zen, seated meditation is called zazen. Za 坐 means “sitting” and Zen 禅 means “meditation”.

The practice of simply sitting, and knowing this is what we are doing, is called shikantaza. Shi 只 means “only”, Kan 管 means “attend to”, Ta 打 means “the act of”, and Za 坐 means “sitting”.

So shikantaza means something like “just sitting”.

Just sitting does not mean sitting in a special way with a special mind. It means sitting here, as we are, without needing to add anything extra.

A friendly approach

When we meditate, the mind wanders. This is natural.

We may find ourselves thinking about work, family, plans, memories, worries, or things we wish had gone differently. This is not a failure of meditation. It is simply what minds do.

When we notice that the mind has wandered, we return gently. We might return to the breath, the body, the sounds around us, or the simple fact of sitting.

There is no need to criticise ourselves. Wandering and returning is part of the practice. We might say it is a feature, not a bug.

How to sit

  1. Choose a quiet place where you can sit comfortably.
  2. Use a chair, sofa, meditation cushion, or bench. All are fine.
  3. Sit in a way that feels steady but not rigid.
  4. Let your hands rest comfortably.
  5. Allow your eyes to be softly open or gently closed.
  6. Take a few breaths and feel the body sitting.
  7. Just sit. Try 5 minutes. Try 10 minutes. Try 20 minutes.

What to do with thoughts

You do not have to stop thinking.

If thoughts come, let them come. If thoughts go, let them go. If the mind is busy, the mind is busy. If the mind becomes quiet, the mind becomes quiet.

The practice is not to win a battle against thought. The practice is to notice, soften, and return.

Over time, this can help us become less caught in our thoughts, and more available to our life.

After sitting

When the period of meditation is finished, take a moment before standing. Feel your hands, feet, breath, and body.

Then return to your ordinary life.

The point of meditation is not to escape ordinary life. It is to meet ordinary life with a little more clarity, kindness, humour, and care.

A saying

“You should meditate for 20 minutes a day. Unless you are too busy. Then you should meditate for an hour a day.” - Unknown Zen Master
